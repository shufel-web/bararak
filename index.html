Banarak Banana Wine – How It’s Made

Equipment Needed

  • 2 Large saucepans
  • 1 Potato masher
  • 1 Ladle
  • 1 Sieve
  • 1 Muslin cloth (or clean tea towel/pillowcase)
  • 1 Funnel
  • 1 Demijohn (4.5L) or fermenting bucket
  • 1 Airlock

Note: All equipment must be sterilised except the saucepans.

Ingredients

  • 1.2 kg ripe bananas (brown/spotty are best)
  • 200 g sultanas
  • 900 g sugar
  • 1 lemon
  • 1 black tea bag
  • Wine yeast

Instructions

  1. Prepare the base: Add about 4 litres of water to a large pan. Roughly chop the bananas (including skins) and add them to the pan with the sultanas.
  2. Simmer: Bring to the boil, then reduce heat and let it simmer.
  3. Prepare tea & lemon: Brew the teabag in a mug of boiling water. Juice the lemon and add the juice to the pan.
  4. Mash: After about 5 minutes of simmering, mash the fruit in the pan. Continue until there are no large chunks. Be careful of hot splashes.
  5. Add tea: Remove the teabag from the mug and add the brewed tea to the pan. Stir well.
  6. Cool slightly: Turn off the heat, cover the pan, and allow the mixture to cool. Leaving it overnight will develop more flavor.
  7. Strain: Strain the liquid into a clean pan using a sieve and muslin cloth. Do not squeeze the pulp through the cloth.
  8. Add sugar & sterilize: Return the strained liquid to heat, bring to a quick boil, then stir in the sugar until dissolved. Turn off the heat.
  9. Cool again: Allow the liquid to cool for about an hour or place the pan in a sink of cold water.
  10. Transfer to fermenter: Pour the cooled liquid into a sterilised demijohn using a funnel. Fit the airlock immediately. Never pour boiling liquid into glass.
  11. Optional – Add pectolase: Add now if using, to help clearing.
  12. Add yeast: Add wine yeast according to package instructions. Leave to ferment in a warm, dark place.

Racking Schedule

  • First rack: After 1 week
  • Second rack: At 3 weeks
  • Third rack: At 6 weeks
  • Final rack: Once fermentation stops

Bottling

Bottle once the wine has cleared and fermentation is fully finished — usually after at least 3 months (6–12 months is better).

Important Warnings

  • Bottling too early can cause bottles to explode.
  • Beginners should use a stabiliser to stop active yeast.
  • Too much sediment at bottling can cause off-flavors.

Aging

For best results, age the bottled wine for at least 1 year before drinking.
